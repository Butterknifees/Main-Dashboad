Risk Management Framework

Demystifying Financial Risk

Risk is not just the chance of losing money; it is the range of possible outcomes. Understanding the math is the first step to mastering it.

CORE METRIC

Standard Deviation & Volatility

Standard Deviation (σ) measures how much your portfolio's returns "wander" away from its average (mean) return. In finance, we call this Volatility.

Higher Volatility = Wider Range of Outcomes = More Uncertainty

If your portfolio has an average daily return of 0.1% and a standard deviation of 1.0%, the "width" of your daily expectations is determined by this 1.0% buffer.

PROBABILITY

The Bell Curve (Normal Distribution)

We assume market returns follow a "Normal Distribution." This creates a mathematical framework for probability:

The 68% Rule

There is a 68% chance that your return will fall within 1 Standard Deviation of the mean.

The 95% Rule

There is a 95% chance that your return will fall within 2 Standard Deviations.

Why it matters: A high Standard Deviation means the "sides" of the mean are further apart, increasing the likelihood of extreme gains or extreme losses.

SCALING

Annualized Risk

Volatility doesn't scale linearly with time; it scales with the square root of time. To convert daily risk to annual risk, we multiply by the square root of trading days (usually 252).

Annual Volatility = Daily Volatility × √252

This is why your dashboard shows an Annualized Volatility of ~13-15% even if your daily moves are only ~0.8%.

INSTITUTIONAL GRADE

Downside & Tail Risk

Value at Risk (VaR)

VaR answers the question: "What is my worst-case loss with 95% confidence?" If your 1-day 95% VaR is 2%, it means there is only a 5% chance you will lose more than 2% in a single day.

Downside Deviation

Standard deviation treats extreme "gains" as risk. Downside Deviation only looks at returns that fall below your target (or zero), focusing purely on the volatility that hurts your portfolio.

Maximum Drawdown

The largest "peak-to-trough" decline your portfolio has ever suffered. This measures your portfolio's resilience during market crashes.
